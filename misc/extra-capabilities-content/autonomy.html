These three capabilities are closely related. Research from the 2018 State of DevOps Report, which has more detail on each of these capabilities, shows that autonomy drives trust, voice, and software delivery and operational performance. Trust and voice, in turn, drive Westrum organizational culture. These relationships are shown in the diagram below.

Autonomy

In autonomous teams, leaders:

  • Establish and communicate goals, but let the team decide how to do the work.
  • Remove roadblocks by keeping rules simple.
  • Let the team change rules if the rules are obstacles to meeting goals.
  • Let the team prioritize good outcomes for customers, even if it means bending the rules.

Giving teams more autonomy drives software delivery performance and leads to feelings of trust and voice, which in turn have all been shown to drive organizational culture.

Trust

Trust reflects how much a person believes their leader or manager is honest, has good motives and intentions, and treats them fairly.

Trust positively affects voice and organizational culture, and is driven by giving your teams more autonomy.

Voice

Voice is how strongly someone feels about their ability and their team's ability to speak up, especially during conflict—for example, when team members disagree, when there are system failures or risks, and when they suggest ideas to improve work.

Voice positively affects organizational culture, and is driven by giving your teams more autonomy and trust.
